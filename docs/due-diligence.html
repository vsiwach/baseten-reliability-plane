Due diligence — the evidence behind the Baseten strategy thesis

Thesis under examination: Reliability at Baseten is something the platform does FOR customers (MCM, internal). The winning move is making it something customers DECLARE and OPERATE — four policy objects (Placement, Failover, Release, SLO) enforced by MCM, operated by agents, sold as a productized 99.99% SLA.

Every claim below carries its source. Items marked ⚠ could not be fully verified. Read §1–§4 as "what is true," §5 as "what we measured ourselves," §6 as "why the thesis follows," §7 as "why it might be wrong."

1 · What the job is actually asking for (verbatim)

From the live posting (jobs.ashbyhq.com/baseten — "Product Manager, Inference Platform," $165K–$330K + equity):

  • "autoscaling to demand (up under load, down to zero when idle) and a single placement policy expressing region, compliance regime, and capacity preference, with compliance-bound workloads given right-of-way on sensitive capacity"
  • "region-aware routing with multi-region / active-active and fallback as first-class policy"
  • "the traffic-shifting that powers canary/shadow/A/B, warm-ups, drain, and probes"
  • "a measurable decline in MTTR through self-serve incident management"

The JD is written in policy language: "placement policy," "first-class policy," "right-of-way." Baseten is telling candidates the destination. The due-diligence question is whether the gap is real — i.e., whether these things genuinely don't exist as customer surfaces today.

2 · What Baseten has today (per capability, sourced)

Autoscaling — mature, self-serve. Seven parameters (min/max replica, window, scale-down delay, max scale-down rate, concurrency target, target utilization), settable via UI or PATCH API; scale-to-zero; wake endpoints; request parking at the router. Source: docs.baseten.co/deployment/autoscaling/overview. Token-based autoscaling (target_in_flight_tokens) exists only on BIS-LLM (their v2 engine): docs.baseten.co/engines/performance-concepts/autoscaling-engines. Gap inside the strength: the docs' own traffic-pattern guide tells users to pre-warm via cron jobs hitting the autoscaling API (docs.baseten.co/deployment/autoscaling/traffic-patterns) — no scheduled or predictive scaling.

Placement / multi-region — capable engine, no customer surface. MCM (Multi-cloud Capacity Management) is a hub-and-spoke global scheduler over Kubernetes workload planes across "20+ clouds," doing placement on geography, priority, and customer constraints, with active-active across clusters and automatic scale-out of failing regions. Sources: baseten.co/blog/how-we-built-multi-cloud-capacity-management/, baseten.co/products/multi-cloud-capacity-management/. Two load-bearing facts from their own material:

  • Region/provider-locking constraints are "manually encoded for each customer" (MCM blog). Placement is a ticket, not a policy object.
  • Regional environments "require initial configuration by Baseten — contact support" and standard endpoints explicitly do NOT guarantee regional routing (docs.baseten.co/deployment/regional-environments).

Priority / right-of-way — nothing customer-visible. The only priority primitive found anywhere in the docs is "sync gets priority over async" (docs.baseten.co/concepts/howbasetenworks). No workload priority classes, no preemption of flexible workloads off compliance-eligible capacity, no notion of "sensitive capacity" pools.

Traffic shifting — promotion-scoped only. Rolling deployments ship (surge/unavailable modes, stabilization windows, pause/resume/cancel/force-complete) but are disabled by default, exist only during a promotion, and are not supported for Chains (docs.baseten.co/deployment/rolling-deployments). The environments docs mention "canary" and "shadow serving" as workflows but there is no standing traffic split, no shadow/mirror primitive, no A/B, no metric-gated auto-rollback anywhere in the docs index (verified against docs.baseten.co/llms.txt).

Warm-up / drain / probes — exists, conservatively tuned. Startup/readiness/liveness probes every 10s with a custom is_healthy() hook; but all three thresholds default to 1800s (30 minutes) — docs call this "deliberately conservative" (docs.baseten.co/development/model/health-checks). A stuck replica can take traffic for up to 30 minutes unless the customer knows to tune it. No declarative warm-up traffic spec; no GPU state snapshots (Modal ships these: modal.com/blog/truly-serverless-gpus).

Observability — metrics yes, operations no. Dashboard metrics + Prometheus export (rate-limited to 6 req/min/org), logs with 7-day export, tracing, audit logs (docs.baseten.co/observability/). No alerting, no SLO objects, no burn-rate rules, no paging integration — absence verified against the full docs index. No per-request cost attribution; request/response size* serves as the token proxy on non-BIS deployments.

Incident management — status page and support. status.baseten.co, restart-tracking graph (June 2026 changelog), SSH into replicas. Customer-triggered remediation = liveness restarts + support tickets. No incident timeline per deployment, no self-serve remediations (replace replica / evacuate region / rollback) as products.

The SLA gap. Marketing: "99.99% uptime for mission-critical workloads," "no downtime, no manual failover" (MCM product page); a customer case study claims "100% uptime" (Rime). Contract: the published SLA commits to 99.9% monthly, and explicitly excludes throttling/queuing on non-reserved capacity and anything beyond provisioned capacity (baseten.co/service-level-agreement/). The engineering appears to over-deliver the contract; nobody has productized the difference.

3 · Why this gap exists now (and why it's the next act, not a criticism)

Baseten built MCM because GPU scarcity forced them to aggregate capacity across clouds — SemiAnalysis reports on-demand rental capacity "sold out across virtually all GPU types" (March 2026, newsletter.semianalysis.com), CoWoS allocated through mid-2027. MCM was built operator-first because that's how you survive scarcity. The Series F blog itself frames what's next: revenue grew 20x, inference volume 40x in a year, >1B calls/day (baseten.co/blog/announcing-our-series-f/; ⚠ "87 clusters / 18 clouds" appears only in secondary press, Baseten's own pages say "20+ clouds"). At that scale, white-glove placement (a human encoding constraints per customer) stops scaling — the manual encoding IS the bottleneck the PM is being hired to productize.

Meanwhile the engine layer is commoditizing upward: NVIDIA Dynamo 1.0 GA'd disaggregated serving and KV-aware routing as open source (developer.nvidia.com/blog/nvidia-dynamo-1-production-ready/), and the Kubernetes Gateway API Inference Extension is standardizing inference-aware routing (docs.nvidia.com/dynamo/integrations). When routing and disaggregation are open standards, differentiation moves up-stack — to placement, capacity, and reliability policy. Compliance regimes harden on the same clock: EU AI Act high-risk obligations fully applicable August 2, 2026 (digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu), and Baseten's marquee vertical is healthcare (Abridge, OpenEvidence — Series F blog), which makes "compliance-bound right-of-way" existential for their own customer base, not hypothetical.

4 · Market check — is anyone already selling this?

  • AWS Bedrock is the closest: cross-region inference profiles (geographic + global) that keep traffic inside a US/EU/APAC boundary with automatic burst routing (docs.aws.amazon.com/bedrock/.../cross-region-inference.html). But it's profile selection, not policy — no capacity preference, no priority classes, on-demand only.
  • Fireworks sells multi-region dedicated deployments with guaranteed capacity (fireworks.ai) — capacity as a SKU, not an operable policy.
  • Together — SLA-backed enterprise tiers, VPC options; no placement/failover policy surface.
  • Modal — best cold starts (GPU memory snapshots), fine autoscaling primitives; limited region control, no multi-cloud failover story.
  • SageMaker — has alarm-gated auto-rollback on deployments (the one release-engine feature ahead of Baseten), at the cost of heavy operational burden.
  • CoreWeave/Anyscale — substrate; the customer owns reliability policy themselves.

Conclusion: nobody sells a customer-operable placement/failover/release/SLO policy layer over multi-cloud capacity. Baseten is uniquely positioned because MCM already enforces all of this internally — the missing piece is the declaration surface and the console. This is "Cloud Infrastructure mid-2000s" (the JD's own analogy): AWS won that era by turning ops into APIs.

6 · Why the thesis follows

The logic chain, stated so it can be attacked:

1. The JD's four outcome areas (placement, active-active/fallback, release engine, MTTR) are all policy-and-console problems, not engine problems — MCM already enforces the behaviors internally (§2). 2. What's missing in every case is the same thing: a customer-visible declaration + observation surface (placement is a ticket, failover is invisible, canary exists only inside promotions, incidents are support cases). 3. Nobody in the market sells that surface (§4); the nearest primitive (Bedrock profiles) is coarse; the engine layer below is commoditizing (§3) — so the surface is where durable differentiation lives. 4. Baseten's contract (99.9%, capacity-scoped) under-promises what MCM's engineering already delivers (99.99% claimed) — a productized SLA converts existing engineering into pricing power. 5. Your own evidence (§5) shows both the pain (friction log) and the payoff (9s MTTR) end-to-end.

Hence the four policy objects — PlacementPolicy (region, compliance regime, capacity preference, right-of-way), FailoverPolicy (multi-region topology, fallback order, drills), ReleasePolicy (canary/shadow/A-B steps, probes, auto-rollback), SLOPolicy (targets that drive scaling, alerting, and agent remediation) — plus the Operate console where those policies and every enforcement decision are visible, and agents execute a closed allowlist of remediations against them.

Why the console is the right first feature (and the right MVP for your application): it's the surface where all four policies become legible; it demos in one screen; it exercises the exact API gaps your friction log documents (#12, #18, #19), so the prototype doubles as evidence; and it maps to the JD's "how customers configure and observe them — end to end."

7 · What could be wrong (counterarguments, honestly)

  • "Customers don't want to operate; they pay Baseten so they don't have to." Partially true — the answer is defaults-with-override (policy objects ship with today's white-glove behavior as default), not raw knobs. The console must be glanceable, not a pager duty.
  • Enterprise-gating may be deliberate margin strategy (BIS-LLM advanced features are sales-gated). Making placement self-serve could cannibalize a high-touch sales motion. Counter: at 20x revenue growth the white-glove path is the bottleneck, and self-serve policy expands the reserved-capacity upsell (capacity preference is a buying surface).
  • MCM internals may not cleanly expose per-customer policy — hub-and-spoke schedulers built operator-first can resist multi-tenancy of intent. This is an engineering-cost risk the PRD must gate with a discovery phase.
  • The 99.99% SLA may be legally hard (capacity exclusions exist because GPU supply is genuinely scarce). Mitigation: SLA tiering bound to reserved capacity + declared multi-region policy — you earn the 9's you configured for.
  • ⚠ Evidence caveats: complaint corpus beyond your own friction log is thin (mostly competitor-authored comparisons); "87 clusters/18 clouds" unverified; Fireworks' rumored $15B round is press talk; the friction log is n=1 workspace on a non-enterprise tier — some walls (A10G org-gating, 10-replica cap) may not exist for paid enterprise accounts.
